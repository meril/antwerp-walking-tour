Antwerp Walking Tour

A Journey Through Art, History & Architecture

January 1, 2026

Tour Itinerary

12 stops • exterior-focused • ~5km

Distance: ~5-6 km Duration: 4-5 hours Terrain: Flat, paved
Suggested Breaks:
  • Coffee: Joe & the Juice (Wiegstraat) before starting, or cafes around Groenplaats (Stop 5)
  • Lunch: Het Eilandje area (Stop 9) has waterfront restaurants — Felix Pakhuis is a converted warehouse
  • Rest: Benches at Het Steen terrace (Stop 8) and Leopold de Waelplaats in Het Zuid (Stop 11)

Tip: Consider splitting into Morning (1-6), then Afternoon (7-9) after coffee. Take tram to Het Zuid (10-11). Skip Stop 12 (Centraal) unless you're heading there.

Morning: The Historic Core

1
Cathedral of Our Lady
Onze-Lieve-Vrouwekathedraal

History

  • Construction began 1352, completed 1521 — 170 years of building
  • Became a cathedral (bishop's seat) in 1559 when the Diocese of Antwerp was established
  • Survived iconoclasm (1566), French Revolutionary looting (1794), and German occupation (1914) — each time losing then recovering treasures
  • The original plan called for two matching towers; only the north tower was completed due to funding issues and changing architectural tastes

Architecture

  • Brabantine Gothic style — characterized by elaborate tracery and vertical emphasis
  • North tower reaches 123 meters — tallest church tower in the Low Countries
  • Seven-aisled nave stretching 118 meters, supported by 125 columns
  • The tower's "lacework" stonework is remarkably delicate for such massive scale — look for the intricate pinnacles and flying buttresses

Art (Interior — visit Jan 2)

  • The Raising of the Cross (1610-11) and The Descent from the Cross (1612-14) — two of Rubens' most dramatic altarpieces
  • Both paintings demonstrate Rubens' mastery of diagonal composition and theatrical lighting
  • The Descent was commissioned by the Guild of Arquebusiers; the Raising originally belonged to St. Walburga's Church
At the Handschoenmarkt (Glove Market) in front, the Brabo Fountain depicts Antwerp's founding legend: the Roman soldier Silvius Brabo cutting off the giant Antigoon's hand and throwing it in the Scheldt — "hand werpen" (hand throwing) became "Antwerpen."
2
Vlaeykensgang
Hidden 16th-century alleyway

History

  • Built in 1591 as housing for cobblers and their families
  • Named after "Vlaeykens" — a common surname or possibly derived from "vlaai" (flat cake)
  • Remained working-class housing for centuries; nearly demolished in the 20th century before preservation efforts saved it
  • Now contains small restaurants and galleries, but retains its authentic medieval character

Architecture

  • Narrow passage only 1-2 meters wide in places — typical of medieval urban planning where space was precious
  • Original cobblestones, some dating to the 16th century
  • Small interior courtyards created light wells for the surrounding buildings
  • Ivy-covered walls and intimate scale create a stark contrast with the busy streets outside
Enter at Oude Koornmarkt 16 — easy to miss if you don't know to look for it. The passage connects to Pelgrimstraat, emerging near the cathedral.
3
Grote Markt
Guild houses & City Hall

History

  • Commercial heart of Antwerp since medieval times — this is where the city's wealth was displayed and business conducted
  • The guild houses represented the economic power of trade associations: coopers, drapers, archers, crossbowmen
  • In the 16th century, Antwerp was the richest city in Europe, handling 40% of world trade — this square shows that wealth
  • The Spanish Fury (1576) destroyed much of the city; many buildings were rebuilt afterward

Architecture

  • Stadhuis (City Hall): Renaissance masterpiece (1561-65) by Cornelis Floris de Vriendt, blending Italian Renaissance with Flemish decoration
  • The City Hall's center bay features a dramatic break from the horizontal rhythm — look for the coat of arms and figures
  • Guild houses: Each competed for attention with gilded statues, elaborate gables, and symbolic imagery representing their trade
  • Look for the Coopers' House (barrel motifs), the Archers' House, and houses with stepped gables vs. curved gables (showing different periods)

Art & Symbolism

  • The Brabo Fountain (1887) by Jef Lambeaux — more dramatic than the version at Handschoenmarkt, showing Brabo mid-throw
  • Guild house facades function as architectural "business cards" — each detail communicates the guild's trade, patron saint, and prosperity
4
Vleeshuis
Butchers' Hall (1504)

History

  • Built 1501-1504 as the guildhall of the butchers — one of the wealthiest and most powerful guilds
  • Butchers had a monopoly on meat sales; only they could sell meat, and only here
  • The building included a great hall for guild meetings, a chapel, and ground-floor meat stalls
  • Now houses the Vleeshuis Museum of music and dance (closed today but reopening)

Architecture

  • Late Gothic with distinctive alternating layers of red brick and white sandstone — creates the striking "bacon" striped effect
  • The layered pattern is both decorative and structural — the sandstone bands help distribute weight
  • Steep stepped gables and corner turrets give it a fortress-like appearance
  • One of the best-preserved examples of a medieval guild hall in the Low Countries
The "bacon stripes" are uniquely Antwerp — you won't see this pattern repeated elsewhere. The building's fortress-like appearance reflected the butchers' wealth and their need to protect valuable meat stores.

Late Morning: The Rubens Quarter

5
Groenplaats
Rubens' original burial site

History & Rubens Context

  • Originally the Cathedral's graveyard — "Groenplaats" means "Green Place," referring to the grass that once covered the graves
  • Rubens was buried here in 1640; his remains were moved inside the Cathedral in 1755 to the family chapel (behind the apse)
  • The statue (1840) by Willem Geefs shows Rubens in diplomat's attire — he was both painter and ambassador, negotiating peace between Spain and England
  • Burials ended in 1784 when Emperor Joseph II banned urban cemeteries — part of Enlightenment-era public health reforms

What to Notice

  • Stand at the statue and look toward the Cathedral tower — this view connects Rubens to his greatest works inside (the altarpieces you'll see on Jan 2)
  • The statue's pedestal shows allegorical figures representing Painting, Architecture, and Fame
  • The square's open layout reflects Enlightenment urban planning — replacing the medieval jumble of graves with rational public space
Rubens died wealthy and celebrated, unlike many artists of his era. His epitaph calls him "the Apelles of his age" — comparing him to Alexander the Great's court painter, the highest compliment 17th-century art could bestow.
6
Rubenshuis
Rubens' home & studio (1610-1640)

History

  • Purchased by Rubens in 1610 after returning from 8 years in Italy; he lived here until his death in 1640
  • Rubens transformed an existing Flemish house into an Italian-style palazzo — designing the renovations himself
  • This was both home and "painting factory" — Rubens employed numerous assistants including Anthony van Dyck and Jacob Jordaens
  • After Rubens' death, the house changed hands and was heavily modified; the city acquired it in 1937 and spent decades restoring it

Architecture

  • The Portico: The ornate Baroque gateway connecting house to studio — one of the few original elements to survive. Designed by Rubens based on Roman triumphal arches
  • The portico features three arched openings, with sculptures representing Mercury and Minerva (commerce and wisdom)
  • The garden pavilion at the back was inspired by Italian Renaissance villas
  • The house shows the transition from Flemish traditional architecture to Italian Baroque influences

Art Context

  • Rubens produced an estimated 1,400 paintings here, with workshop assistance
  • The studio had large windows (facing north for consistent light) and high ceilings to accommodate massive canvases
  • Rubens also amassed an important art collection here, which he used for study and as models
The historic residence is under renovation until 2030, but the garden and Rubens Experience are accessible (closed Jan 1). The new entrance is at Hopland 13.

Afternoon: River & Port Heritage

7
Sint-Carolus Borromeus
Baroque Jesuit church (1621)

History

  • Built 1615-1621 by the Jesuits as part of the Counter-Reformation — designed to overwhelm and inspire
  • Rubens was closely involved in the design, particularly the facade and tower
  • Originally had 39 ceiling paintings by Rubens — all destroyed by lightning fire in 1718
  • Named after Carlo Borromeo, the Counter-Reformation archbishop of Milan, canonized in 1610

Architecture

  • The facade is considered one of the finest Baroque facades in the Low Countries — theatrical, dynamic, designed to impress
  • Three-story design with dramatic interplay of columns, pilasters, and broken pediments
  • The tower combines Baroque and Gothic elements — showing regional adaptation of Italian ideas
  • The interior uses marble, gilt, and dramatic lighting to create emotional impact — architecture as religious propaganda

Art

  • The Lady Chapel retains two paintings by Rubens that survived the 1718 fire
  • The church demonstrates how Baroque art and architecture worked together to create immersive religious experiences
Jan 1 Note: Church only open for Mass (11:30am Artists' Mass, 5pm Parish Mass) — no tourist visits. But Hendrik Conscienceplein, the square in front, is one of the most photogenic spots in Antwerp — the church facade framed by historic buildings.
8
Het Steen & Scheldt Riverfront
Medieval fortress & the river that built Antwerp

Het Steen — The Fortress

  • The oldest building in Antwerp — parts date to the 10th century, with major rebuilding in the 1520s under Emperor Charles V
  • Originally a fortress protecting the river crossing; later served as a prison (until 1823)
  • The name "Steen" simply means "stone" — a reference to its stone construction when most buildings were wood
  • What survives is the gatehouse of a much larger medieval castle complex
  • The relief above the entrance (1520) shows Semini, a Scandinavian fertility god — possibly repurposed from a Viking-era stone

The Scheldt — Antwerp's Lifeline

  • The Scheldt River made Antwerp — navigable from the North Sea, it allowed ocean-going ships to reach the city
  • By the 16th century, Antwerp was the busiest port in the world, handling 40% of world trade: spices, textiles, gems, and art
  • The river was also Antwerp's vulnerability — the Dutch blockade of the Scheldt (1585-1863) devastated the city's economy for nearly 300 years
  • Look across to Linkeroever (Left Bank) — the contrast between medieval Steen and modern riverfront captures Antwerp's layered history

Folklore

  • The statue of Lange Wapper (Long Wapper) depicts a shape-shifting giant from Antwerp folklore who terrorized drunkards leaving the waterfront taverns — a 16th-century cautionary tale
Stand on the terrace and look north toward the port — you're seeing the continuation of 1,000+ years of river trade. The modern cranes in the distance handle some of Europe's largest cargo volumes.
9
MAS & Het Eilandje
Museum aan de Stroom & old docks

History

  • Het Eilandje ("Little Island") was Antwerp's 19th-century port district — warehouses, docks, and maritime industry
  • Declined in the 20th century as port activities moved north; became derelict and largely abandoned
  • Redeveloped since the 2000s as a mixed-use neighborhood — a model of adaptive reuse
  • The MAS opened in 2011, designed to tell the story of Antwerp's connection to the world through trade, migration, and cultural exchange

Architecture

  • MAS building: Designed by Neutelings Riedijk (2011) — 60 meters tall, clad in red Indian sandstone and curved glass
  • The stacked, rotating volumes evoke shipping containers or hands (the Antwerp symbol)
  • The facade includes 3,185 cast aluminum hands — referencing the Brabo legend
  • External escalators zigzag up through glass-enclosed passages with views at each level — the boulevard and rooftop panorama are free

Nearby

  • Red Star Line Museum: In the former inspection buildings where 2 million emigrants departed for America (1873-1934), including many Jewish refugees
  • Bonapartedok & Willemdok: The old dock basins, now surrounded by converted warehouses and modern apartments
Jan 1 Note: MAS is completely closed on New Year's Day — including rooftop and escalators. Still worth walking around the building exterior to see the architecture and the hand motifs. The surrounding dock area and Red Star Line building exteriors are accessible.

Late Afternoon: Het Zuid

~2km from MAS (Stop 9) to KMSKA (Stop 10). Tram 9 or 15 from Napoleonkaai, or taxi.

10
KMSKA (Exterior)
Royal Museum of Fine Arts

History

  • Founded 1810 under Napoleon; the current building opened 1890
  • The collection began with artworks owned by the Antwerp Guild of Saint Luke (active 1382-1773)
  • Expanded dramatically through the 1840 bequest of former mayor Florent van Ertborn, who collected Early Netherlandish art when it was unfashionable
  • Closed 2011-2022 for major renovation; reopened with 40% more exhibition space

Architecture

  • Neoclassical design by Jean-Jacques Winders and Frans Van Dijk (1884-1894)
  • The facade features bronze horse-drawn chariots by Thomas Vinçotte at the roofline
  • Seven medallion portraits of artists including Jan van Eyck and Rubens
  • The 2022 renovation by KAAN Architects added a modern wing for contemporary art while preserving the historic galleries

Art (Interior — visit Jan 2)

  • Jan van Eyck: Saint Barbara (1437) — an unfinished masterpiece showing his technique; Madonna at the Fountain
  • Rogier van der Weyden: Seven Sacraments Altarpiece, Portrait of Philip de Croy
  • Rubens: Major works including Adoration of the Magi
  • James Ensor: The world's largest collection of his work
  • Current exhibitions: Magritte retrospective (until Feb 2026), Marthe Donas/Archipenko (until Jan 11)
11
Het Zuid Neighborhood
19th-century boulevards & galleries

History

  • Developed in the late 19th century after the demolition of the old Spanish citadel (built 1567 to control the city after rebellions)
  • Designed as a bourgeois residential quarter with wide boulevards and grand townhouses
  • The area declined in the mid-20th century, then was rediscovered by artists and gallerists in the 1980s-90s
  • Now Antwerp's gallery district, with a more bohemian feel than the tourist center

Architecture

  • Leopold de Waelplaats and surrounding streets show eclectic 19th-century styles — Neo-Renaissance, Neo-Gothic, early Art Nouveau
  • The Gedempte Zuiderdokken (filled-in southern docks) create a long, linear park space
  • Look for townhouse details: ornate ironwork, carved stone facades, distinctive doorways
Good area for dinner — more local and less touristy than Grote Markt. Wander and see what appeals.
12
Antwerp Centraal Station
The "Railway Cathedral"

History

  • Built 1895-1905 to replace an earlier wooden station, demonstrating Belgium's pride in its railway network (the first in continental Europe)
  • Designed by Louis Delacenserie (station building) with the iron train hall by Clément Van Bogaert
  • King Leopold II was closely involved, wanting a statement of Belgian prestige
  • Often called the "Railway Cathedral" for its monumental, almost religious grandeur

Architecture

  • Eclectic style combining Neo-Baroque, Neo-Renaissance, and Beaux-Arts influences
  • The main hall features a massive dome (75 meters high) with elaborate stonework, marble, and gilt details
  • Over 20 types of marble and stone were used in the interior
  • The iron and glass train hall (185m × 66m) spans the platforms without intermediate supports — an engineering marvel of its time
  • Recent renovations (1998-2009) added underground levels for high-speed trains while preserving the historic structure
Even if you're not taking a train, walk inside to see the main hall. The dramatic interior rivals many cathedrals — fitting for a city that worshiped commerce.
